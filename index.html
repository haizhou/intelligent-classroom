What If Every Student Had a Brilliant Tutor Available 24/7?

Inside the agentic AI systems that are turning Bloom’s impossible dream (personalised one-on-one tutoring for every learner) into an engineering problem with a real solution.

20 min read ~5,000 words Emerging Topics in Integrated Machine Learning Systems 25/26
Part 1

Education at a Crossroads

TL;DR

Bloom proved in 1984 that personalised tutoring produces transformative results. Forty years later, the world still needs 44 million more teachers just to reach basic coverage — and the ones we have are leaving. Every attempt to close that gap technologically, from MOOCs to early adaptive platforms, fell short for the same reason: they could deliver content, but they could not teach. Agentic AI is the first technology that credibly can. Khanmigo already has 700,000 users, and a systematic review of 82 studies confirms real learning gains. Part 2 explains the six technologies that make it work.

1.1 The Answer We’ve Had Since 1984

In 1984, Benjamin Bloom published a finding that should have changed everything. Students receiving one-on-one tutoring consistently performed two standard deviations above their conventionally taught peers — a gap so large it became its own shorthand: the “two-sigma problem.” The implication was unambiguous. Personalised instruction, delivered by a tutor who knows you, adapts to you, and asks you why rather than just whether — it works, reliably and dramatically. The problem was equally unambiguous: there are not enough tutors, and there has never been enough money.

“The search for methods of group instruction as effective as one-to-one tutoring — that is the ‘2 sigma problem.’”

Benjamin Bloom, Educational Researcher, 1984

Forty years later, the structural reality makes Bloom’s finding feel even more remote. The world now needs 44 million additional teachers by 2030 just to achieve universal basic education (UNESCO, 2024). In the United States, one in eight teaching positions was unfilled or staffed by an under-qualified teacher in 2024–25 (Learning Policy Institute, 2025). In Sub-Saharan Africa, the student-to-teacher ratio can exceed 56:1 — at which point personalised instruction is not merely unaffordable; it is physically impossible. And the pipeline is collapsing from the other end too: one in five teachers under 30 plans to leave the profession within five years (TALIS, 2024), with burnout, workload, and stagnating pay cited as the driving forces. The crisis is not a shortage of evidence about what works. It is a shortage of the people and resources needed to deliver it.

1.2 The Attempts That Fell Short

The decades since Bloom were not short of ambition. MOOCs burst onto the scene in the early 2010s with world-changing promises: top-university lectures, free, for anyone with an internet connection. The reality was more modest. Completion rates rarely exceeded 10%, and the experience was fundamentally passive — watching videos and clicking through quizzes is not the same as learning. Adaptive platforms like early Khan Academy and Carnegie Learning went further, adding personalisation through item-response algorithms. But they remained confined to predefined content paths and rigid rule-based logic. They could make a problem slightly harder if you got the last one right. They could not ask you why you got it wrong. The gap between what Bloom proved and what technology could actually do remained stubbornly, frustratingly wide.

1.3 Enter Agentic AI: From Chatbot to Pedagogical Partner

The difference between a chatbot and an agentic AI is the difference between a search engine and a research assistant. A chatbot responds to queries; an agentic system pursues goals. It plans, reasons, uses tools, and adapts its approach based on what is working. In education, that means a tutor that does not just answer your question. It notices you are confused, traces the confusion to a specific misconception, retrieves a relevant example from your textbook, and walks you through it with Socratic questions (Schneider, 2025; Acharya et al., 2025).

“Agentic AI refers to systems that can act autonomously to achieve goals without the need for constant human guidance. The system understands the goal and the context to the problem the user is trying to solve.”

Purdy, Harvard Business Review, 2024

This is not theoretical. Khan Academy’s Khanmigo grew from roughly 68,000 users during its 2023–24 pilot to over 700,000 in 2024–25, with projections exceeding one million for 2025–26 (Khan Academy, 2025; DiCerbo, 2025). A systematic review of 82 peer-reviewed studies confirms that the field has moved well beyond proof-of-concept: agentic tutors are now deployed at scale, producing measurable learning gains across subjects and contexts (Garcia-Bosque et al., 2025). The question is no longer whether AI can enhance education. It is which technologies make it work — and what happens when they come together.

Part 2

The Tech Stack Behind the Intelligent Classroom

TL;DR

Building a truly effective AI tutor means solving six distinct engineering problems in sequence. How does it reason carefully rather than guess? How do you ensure what it says is both grounded in your course and mathematically correct? How does it communicate through more than text? How does it remember a student across months? How do you split the work across specialised agents? And how do you make all of this run fast enough for real conversation on a Chromebook? Each question has an answer. Together, they add up to something qualitatively new.

2.1 How Should an AI Think? From Guessing to Reasoning

Start with the most basic question: what separates an AI tutor from a search engine with good manners? A search engine retrieves. A tutor reasons — it holds a model of the student’s understanding, identifies where that model has gaps, and constructs a response calibrated to close them. The engineering question is how to get a language model to do this reliably, rather than pattern-matching to the most statistically plausible answer.

“Humans tend to think for longer on difficult problems to reliably improve their decisions. Can we instill a similar capability into today’s large language models?”

Snell et al., ICLR 2025

Chain-of-thought prompting (Wei et al., 2023) was the first serious step: prompt the model to reason step-by-step before committing to an answer, and performance on mathematics and logic improves substantially. ReAct (Yao et al., 2023) went further, interleaving reasoning with tool use — the agent pauses mid-thought to query a knowledge base or run a calculation, then continues with that new information incorporated. Both approaches, however, still operated within a fixed computational budget. Whatever the question, the model got the same amount of compute.

That changed with test-time compute scaling. OpenAI’s o1 and o3 demonstrated that you can make a model substantially smarter not by making it bigger, but by giving it more time to think at inference. A smaller model given a longer reasoning budget can outperform one fourteen times larger running under standard decoding (Snell et al., ICLR 2025). For an AI tutor, this reframes the design problem entirely. A vocabulary check and an olympiad proof do not need the same compute. The system can allocate thinking time dynamically — fast for recall, deliberate for derivation — in a way that mirrors how a skilled teacher actually works.

2.2 Can We Trust What It Says? Grounding and Correctness

Good reasoning is necessary but not sufficient. A model that thinks carefully can still think carefully about the wrong thing — producing a hallucination with the same deliberate confidence it brings to a correct answer. And in a tutoring context, this matters more than in most: a student who cannot yet verify the output has no defence against a plausible-sounding error. The architecture needs a second layer of enforcement — not just how the model reasons, but what it reasons from, and whether the computations it produces are actually right.

“Across 82 studies, five themes recurred. The first: retrieval grounding dramatically reduces hallucination — not prompt engineering, not fine-tuning, but anchoring generation in verified source material.”

Garcia-Bosque et al., Information, 2025 — systematic review of LLM agents in education

The grounding problem comes first. A model trained on the entire internet has no particular reason to privilege your department’s lecture notes over a decade-old Reddit thread. Retrieval-augmented generation attacks this directly: before the model speaks, fetch verified passages from a curated corpus and inject them as context, constraining generation to what your course actually says. But early dense-vector RAG had a structural flaw in education — it retrieved semantically similar text without understanding prerequisite dependencies. A student asking about Newton’s second law might receive an accurate passage about force, but without the scaffolding connecting it to momentum and rate of change, the answer lands in a vacuum.

GraphRAG closes this gap by replacing the flat document store with a structured concept network. If a student struggles with \(F=ma\), the knowledge graph maps this node to Mass, Acceleration, and from there back to Velocity and Momentum — returning not just the answer, but the entire cognitive scaffolding needed to reach it. A two-stage retrieval pipeline sharpens precision further: BM25 casts a wide net in milliseconds; a cross-encoder reranker then rescores every candidate with full attention, surfacing what is genuinely most relevant rather than merely most similar. The Garcia-Bosque et al. (2025) review identifies retrieval grounding as the single most effective hallucination-reduction technique across 82 studies — not prompt engineering, not fine-tuning, but anchoring generation in verified source material.

$$ P(y \mid x) = \sum_{z \in \mathcal{Z}} P(z \mid x)\, P(y \mid z, x) $$

The RAG objective: marginalise over retrieved documents \(z\). Retrieval probability \(P(z \mid x)\) is computed via dense vector similarity; generation \(P(y \mid z, x)\) is conditioned on both query and retrieved context. BM25 coarse recall followed by cross-encoder reranking substantially improves what actually reaches the model.

Grounding solves the provenance problem. But it does not solve correctness — and in STEM, these are different things entirely. A retrieved passage can be perfectly accurate and still leave a student with a wrong answer, because the model that reads it may still make an algebraic error when applying the concept. Ask a frontier language model to simplify a non-trivial expression and it will often produce something plausible-looking but subtly wrong. The confidence is indistinguishable from the correctness.

The solution is neurosymbolic reasoning: a principled division of labour between what neural models do well and what symbolic systems do well. The LLM parses student intent, chooses pedagogical framing, and generates natural-language explanation. The symbolic solver — SymPy for algebra, Z3 for logical constraints, Lean 4 for formal proof verification — handles deterministic computation whose correctness is guaranteed by execution, not inferred from confidence. The SymCode framework (2025) makes this concrete: it translates a student’s natural-language problem into Python and SymPy, executes it, and feeds any errors back into a self-debugging loop until the computation succeeds. LIPS (ICLR 2025) extends this to competition mathematics, partitioning the tactic space so enumerable algebraic lemmas go to symbolic modules and open-ended rewriting goes to the LLM — achieving state-of-the-art results on Olympiad-level inequalities without any additional training data.

2.3 Beyond Text: How the Tutor Sees, Hears, and Shows

The pipeline now reasons carefully and produces verified answers from your course materials. What it still cannot do is perceive the full context in which learning actually happens. Students do not only type questions — they speak them, sketch diagrams on paper, point at confusing passages, and signal frustration through expressions they may not consciously control. An AI tutor that processes only text is reading a partial transcript of a much richer interaction.

Foundation models like GPT-4o, Gemini 2.5, and Claude now process text, speech, and vision natively. Researchers at the NSF iSAT Institute (University of Colorado) have fine-tuned a Discrete Multimodal LLM specifically for classroom speech, tackling background noise, children’s pronunciation patterns, and the diverse accents that off-the-shelf ASR handles poorly (Trinh et al., 2025). The vision pipeline, meanwhile, lets the system analyse a handwritten free-body diagram and identify the specific conceptual error visible in the sketch — not just in what the student typed.

But the more consequential development is not passive perception — it is active generation. Abstract concepts in STEM resist text description in a way that becomes obvious the moment you try to explain \(PV=nRT\) without drawing anything. The most advanced agentic tutors are beginning to generate interactive environments on the fly: not static diagrams, but manipulable simulations where the student changes a variable and immediately sees what happens to everything else. The equation stops being something to memorise and starts being something to discover.

2.4 Does It Remember You? The Persistent Memory Problem

A tutor that reasons carefully, says the right things, and communicates through voice and diagrams is still fundamentally limited if it begins every session from scratch. Real teaching is cumulative. A good teacher knows that this particular student has been confusing correlation with causation since Unit 2, learns best through examples before theory, and made a breakthrough on integration by parts last Thursday. Without that accumulated understanding, every session is a cold start — competent, perhaps, but not personal, and not pedagogically coherent over time.

This is the persistent memory problem, and it is the frontier that 2025–2026 research is most actively addressing. The challenge is architectural: LLMs operate within a fixed context window, and everything outside it is simply inaccessible. Prepending a summary of past sessions quickly degrades as weeks of interaction compress into a lossy paragraph that loses the nuance that made it useful.

“Existing solutions remain fundamentally limited. Most rely on flat, narrowly scoped memory components, constraining their ability to personalise, abstract, and reliably recall user-specific information over time.”

Wang & Chen, ‘MIRIX: Multi-Agent Memory System for LLM-Based Agents,’ 2025

The more sophisticated approach treats the LLM itself as an operating system managing its own memory tiers. MemGPT (Packer et al., 2023; now Letta) introduced the core insight: the model decides what to store, summarise, and forget — using tool calls to move information between fast in-context working memory and a slower external archival store, exactly as an OS pages between RAM and disk. More recent architectures push this further: MIRIX (2025) separates memory into six distinct types — Core, Episodic, Semantic, Procedural, Resource, and Knowledge Vault — each managed by a dedicated sub-agent, delivering up to 26% improvement on conversational benchmarks with 91% lower latency than full-context approaches.

For education, this maps onto the student model concept from Intelligent Tutoring Systems: a dynamic, continuously updated representation of what a specific learner knows, where they struggle, and how their understanding evolves. The gap between a stateless chatbot and a persistent-memory tutor is not merely technical. It is the gap between a substitute teacher who does not know your name and a mentor who has been paying attention for months.

2.5 One Agent Is Not Enough: Multi-Agent Specialisation

Even with memory, a single agent faces a structural tension: the same system that diagnoses what a student does not understand also has to design the remediation plan, adapt the next exercise, and evaluate whether the intervention worked. These are distinct cognitive tasks. Collapsing them into one agent is like asking a doctor to also run their own lab, write their own referrals, and manage their own appointments — technically possible, but worse at all of them.

Wang et al. (2025b) demonstrate a five-agent architecture where each role is narrow and focused: a Gap Identifier diagnoses knowledge deficits; a Learner Profiler models the student’s characteristics and pace; a Dynamic Planner generates a personalised learning path; a Content Adapter calibrates difficulty and modality; and a Feedback Synthesiser aggregates assessment signals into a coherent picture. Agents run in parallel where possible, with outputs reconciled by an orchestrating layer. Less “one very clever tutor,” more “a well-coordinated department.”

Underlying all of this is the question of how the system actually knows what a student knows. Static test scores are a blunt instrument. Bayesian Knowledge Tracing (BKT) models each skill as a hidden binary — known or not known — updated by observed responses. Deep Knowledge Tracing (DKT) replaces the binary with a continuous latent representation learned by an LSTM, capturing richer dependencies between skills. The most recent work goes further still: Scarlatos et al. (2025) use the tutoring dialogue itself as the assessment instrument, refining knowledge estimates through conversation rather than formal tests. A well-designed agentic tutor is always assessing, even when it appears to be simply talking.

2.6 Can It Actually Run? Deployment, Compression, and the Two-Second Wall

The architecture described above is only useful if it can run on hardware students actually have, and respond fast enough to sustain a conversation. These are not implementation details — they are the difference between a system that exists in research papers and one that exists in classrooms. Two distinct problems need solving: the memory wall that prevents large models from running on constrained devices, and the latency wall that determines whether the interaction feels like dialogue or like waiting for a page to load.

The hardware problem is the bottleneck. State-of-the-art LLMs contain tens of billions of parameters, and running them requires GPU memory far beyond what Chromebooks, shared tablets, and underpowered lab PCs provide. The constraint is not compute but bandwidth — the cost of shuttling billions of weights between DRAM and processor on every inference pass. Quantization attacks this directly, compressing each weight from its 32-bit floating-point representation into 4 or 8 bits:

$$ W_q = \text{round}\!\left(\frac{W_{\text{fp}}}{S}\right) + Z $$

Modern calibration-aware methods like AWQ and GPTQ select \(S\) and \(Z\) to minimise task-specific accuracy loss. 4-bit quantized models retain over 91% benchmark accuracy while cutting memory footprint by up to 8× (Agrawal et al., 2025). Knowledge distillation compresses capability (85% retained at a fraction of the size); structured pruning removes attention heads and layers that contribute least to output quality, shrinking models by up to 60% without retraining (Cai et al., 2026).

Getting the model running is only half the problem. Human conversation has a hard latency threshold — a pause longer than two seconds reads as incompetence, and for a voice-based tutor this is not a preference but a constraint. The end-to-end budget decomposes as:

$$ T_{\text{total}} = T_{\text{edge}}^{\text{infer}} + T_{\text{net}}^{\text{tx}} + T_{\text{cloud}}^{\text{infer}} + T_{\text{net}}^{\text{rx}} $$

Each term is a distinct engineering target. Edge inference is minimised by quantization and pruning. Network transmission is minimised by sending compact feature vectors rather than raw waveforms — compressing upstream payload by an order of magnitude. Cloud inference is where speculative decoding makes the decisive difference.

Edge-cloud co-design splits the pipeline sensibly: latency-critical, privacy-sensitive tasks (speech detection, engagement scoring, feature extraction) run on-device, with biometric data never leaving the hardware. The heavy reasoning runs in the cloud. Speculative decoding then compresses cloud inference time directly. The insight is that LLM generation is memory-bound, not compute-bound — GPUs sit mostly idle between tokens because each step requires loading billions of weights for a single pass. Speculative decoding breaks this by pairing a small, fast draft model with the large target model: the draft proposes a token sequence, the target verifies the entire sequence in one parallel pass, accepts the correct prefix, and regenerates only from the first mismatch. When draft quality is high, multiple tokens are committed per verification step, and wall-clock throughput rises 2–3× with zero degradation in output quality. Recent work on edge-distributed speculative decoding achieves up to 44.9% latency reduction on heterogeneous networks (Zhu et al., 2025) — making the two-second SLA consistently crossable in practice.

Six problems. Six answers. The system that emerges reasons adaptively rather than pattern-matching, grounds itself in verified knowledge and checks its own computations, perceives and communicates through voice and vision and interactive simulation, remembers a specific student across months of learning, coordinates specialised expertise rather than averaging over it, and responds fast enough to feel like a real conversation. None of these capabilities existed in combination five years ago. The next part asks not whether the system works — but whether we are ready for what it means.

Part 3

The Hard Questions We Can’t Afford to Ignore

TL;DR

Three risks sit beneath the promise of agentic AI in education — and they are not equal. Privacy has an architectural solution: edge computing keeps the most sensitive data off servers entirely. The digital divide is a structural problem that technology alone cannot fix; the third wave of exclusion is not about devices but about whether anyone around you knows how to use them well. And the deepest risk is the one least discussed — an AI that answers every question may, quietly, be eroding the student’s capacity to ask better ones.

3.1 The Data Problem: Knowing Everything About a Child

To personalise well, an AI tutor needs to know a lot. What a student finds confusing. How they respond to frustration. When they disengage. What learning pace suits them best. Build a rich enough model of a learner, and you can genuinely transform their education. Build the same model without proper safeguards, and you have created something else entirely: a detailed psychological dossier on a child.

The tension is not incidental — it is structural. The richer the learner model, the better the tutoring and the more dangerous any breach or misuse becomes. A profile capturing a child’s misconceptions, emotional patterns, and behavioural tendencies could, if compromised or commercialised, enable discrimination in future educational selection or employment. A 2025 systematic review of AI in classroom management found that only 22% of 104 studies even addressed ethical concerns — a striking absence given the sensitivity of the data involved. UNESCO (2025) warns that without equitable safeguards, AI-powered education risks deepening the very inequalities it claims to solve.

“Without proper safeguards — strong data protection, ethical frameworks, transparent governance, inclusive access policies — the right to education and other human rights are at risk.”

UNESCO, AI and Education: Protecting the Rights of Learners, 2025

The EU AI Act, which classifies educational AI as “high-risk,” mandates transparency, human oversight, and data governance. But regulation is necessarily retrospective. The more durable protection comes from design. Privacy-by-design — data minimisation, purpose limitation, algorithmic explainability — has to be baked into the architecture before deployment, not bolted on afterwards.

Here is where the engineering of Part 2 pays an unexpected dividend. The edge computing architectures built to reduce latency also provide a physical guarantee of privacy. When speech recognition, engagement scoring, and preliminary NLP run on the student’s own device, the most sensitive biometric signals — a child’s voice, facial micro-expressions, real-time behavioural patterns — never reach a server at all. No legal framework is as leak-proof as data that never travels. This is not a coincidence. It is the rarest kind of engineering outcome: a solution that optimises for two objectives at once, and where the optimisation for one makes the other harder to violate.

Privacy, in other words, is the hard question with the clearest answer. The next two are harder.

3.2 The Divide That Moves: Why AI Could Make Inequality Worse

The optimistic case for AI tutoring is essentially an equity argument. If every student can have access to a patient, knowledgeable tutor at any hour, then geography and income stop determining educational fate. Bloom’s two-sigma advantage, which has historically been the preserve of the wealthy, becomes universally available. The numbers are compelling: Khanmigo at 1.4 million users, Squirrel AI at ten million. Something real is happening.

But equity arguments about technology have a troubling track record. MOOCs were supposed to democratise elite university education. The students who benefited most were those who were already educated. Smartphones were supposed to bridge the digital divide. The gap in meaningful usage by socioeconomic status has barely moved. There is a consistent pattern worth taking seriously: access to a tool does not translate to benefit from a tool without the surrounding conditions that make the tool usable.

“The third digital divide is not about access to technology. It is about access to the people who know how to use it well.”

Trucano, Brookings Institution, 2024

Brookings (2024) mapped this pattern onto AI with precision, identifying what they call the three waves of the digital divide. The first is the one we talk about most: access. As of 2024, 2.6 billion people remain offline (ITU, 2024). Internet reaches 83% of urban areas globally but just 48% of rural ones. The second divide is skill: having a connection does not mean knowing how to use it effectively for learning. But it is the third divide that is both the newest and the most insidious: guided use. Affluent students get AI tools and teachers and parents who coach them on how to extract value from those tools. Disadvantaged students get the tools alone, without the interpretive scaffolding. The gap this creates is not visible in connectivity statistics. It does not show up in device ownership data. It is invisible until you look at outcomes.

The implication is sobering. Simply distributing AI tutors without simultaneously investing in teacher training, multilingual models, culturally responsive content, and basic infrastructure does not narrow the gap — it widens it in a way that is harder to see and therefore harder to address. The technology, absent the surrounding human capital, does not equalise. It amplifies. And the students bearing the cost of that amplification are the ones who can least afford it.

3.3 The Quiet Erosion: What Happens to Students Who Stop Thinking for Themselves

This is the question that the discourse around AI in education has been slowest to confront. Not whether students will cheat — they will, and institutions are adapting — but something more fundamental: what happens to the way students think when they grow up with a system that can answer every question faster and more fluently than any human they know?

The cognitive offloading research gives a first answer. Gerlich (2025) studied 666 participants and found a clear relationship: the more frequently people used AI tools, the worse they scored on measures of critical thinking, with younger participants showing the strongest effect. The mechanism is not mysterious. Memory consolidation depends on effortful encoding. When a tool removes the effort, it also removes the consolidation. The forgetting curve steepens. The knowledge does not stick — because the mind was never really asked to hold it.

“AI-dependent users scored significantly lower on critical thinking and showed steeper forgetting curves. The more they relied on AI, the less they retained.”

Gerlich, ‘ChatGPT as a Cognitive Crutch,’ 2025

Coeckelbergh, Social Epistemology, 2025

But cognitive offloading is only the surface of the problem. The deeper issue is epistemic — about who gets to say what counts as knowledge, and how that authority gets established. Jose et al. (2025) describe AI as a surrogate knower: a fluent, confident voice that students increasingly treat as authoritative, not because they have assessed its reasoning but because it sounds like it knows. The problem is not that the AI is always wrong. It is that it is often right in a way that forecloses the question of why. A student who has learned to ask “what does the AI say?” before forming their own view is being trained, subtly, in intellectual deference.

This matters more in education than in most other contexts, because school is precisely where students are supposed to be developing their capacity to assess claims, disagree with authority, and form independent judgements. A 2025 study of 166 Norwegian high school students found an emerging pattern the researchers called AI individualism: young people increasingly turning to social AI for emotional support, personal decisions, and creative guidance — not just answers to homework questions. The AI was functioning as something closer to a confidant than a calculator (Springer, 2025). When the entity you confide in has no values of its own, but was trained on data that reflects the values of its builders, the question of whose worldview is being quietly transmitted becomes urgent.

Tanchuk and Taylor (2025) put the sharpest point on this. They argue that the standard model of AI tutoring — a system that monitors what you do, adapts to your responses, and decides what you should learn next, without being subject to your scrutiny in return — is structurally opposed to what they call epistemic reciprocity: the norm that those who teach are also accountable to those they teach, that authority is justified rather than merely asserted, that questioning flows in both directions. An AI tutor that students cannot interrogate, whose training data they cannot audit, and whose pedagogical decisions they cannot contest is not a neutral tool. It is a new kind of epistemic authority — one with no obligation to explain itself.

The answer is not to abandon AI tutoring. It is to design it with this risk explicitly in mind: Socratic scaffolding rather than direct answers, transparency about uncertainty and source, and — perhaps most importantly — a curriculum that treats AI literacy itself as a foundational skill. Students who understand how these systems work, where they fail, and why they produce the outputs they do are far less susceptible to the quiet erosion of their intellectual autonomy. The goal is not students who use AI well. It is students who can tell the difference between using AI and being used by it.

Conclusions

The Two-Sigma Problem, Forty Years Later

In 1984, Benjamin Bloom proved that one-on-one tutoring could lift student performance by two standard deviations above the classroom average. The result was electrifying, and then, for four decades, frustrating. Everyone knew personalised tutoring worked. Nobody could afford to provide it at scale. For the first time, the convergence of large language models, agentic architectures, retrieval-augmented generation, multi-agent orchestration, and multimodal perception offers a credible technological pathway to that goal. Millions of students are already interacting with AI tutors daily. The question is no longer whether this technology works. It is whether we are deploying it wisely.

The evidence reviewed here points to a critical qualification that should temper any utopian impulse: hybrid human-AI systems consistently outperform fully autonomous ones. The most effective implementations position AI as infrastructure for teachers and students, not a replacement for the relationships at the heart of education. Privacy has an architectural solution. The digital divide has a policy solution, though one that demands investment the market alone will not provide. But the erosion of epistemic autonomy — the quiet displacement of a student’s own reasoning by a system fluent enough to make that displacement feel natural — is a risk that no single regulation or design pattern fully resolves. It requires ongoing vigilance at every level: curriculum, pedagogy, platform design, and institutional governance.

“AI offers major opportunities for education, provided that its deployment in schools is guided by clear ethical principles. This technology must complement the human and social dimensions of learning — it must become a tool at the service of teachers and pupils.”

Audrey Azoulay, UNESCO Director-General, 2025

Three open questions will shape what comes next. First, longitudinal evidence: almost all existing evaluations span fewer than eight weeks. The long-term effects of sustained AI tutoring on learning, motivation, and cognitive development remain genuinely unknown — and unknowable until we run the studies. Second, epistemic design: the field needs a shared framework for what it means to build an AI tutor that strengthens rather than supplants independent thinking — what metrics to use, what architectures to favour, and what commercial incentives to resist. Third, governance that keeps pace: AI deployment in education is outrunning the regulatory institutions designed to oversee it. The EU AI Act and UNESCO’s Ethics of AI Recommendation are necessary starts, but international coordination needs to move faster than product cycles.

The promise of agentic AI in education is not that it will make teachers obsolete. It is that it will make the best practices of teaching — individualised attention, Socratic dialogue, formative assessment, adaptive scaffolding — available at a scale that was previously impossible. Bloom’s two-sigma problem may finally have a solution. But the path there runs through equity, through epistemic humility, and through a stubborn insistence that the goal of education is not students who can retrieve answers. It is students who know how to think.
