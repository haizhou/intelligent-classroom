Part 1

Education at a Crossroads

1.1 The Global Education Crisis

Education systems worldwide are confronting a crisis of capacity. According to UNESCO and the Teacher Task Force, the world needs approximately 44 million additional teachers by 2030 to achieve universal basic education (UNESCO, 2024). The 2024 Teaching and Learning International Survey (TALIS) found that one in five teachers under 30 plans to leave the profession within five years, a figure that rises to nearly half in some countries (Education International, 2025). In the United States alone, the Learning Policy Institute estimates that roughly 411,500 teaching positions were either unfilled or occupied by teachers not fully certified for their assignments in the 2024–25 school year, representing approximately one in eight positions nationally (Learning Policy Institute, 2025).

The consequences extend beyond staffing numbers. Teacher burnout and attrition create a vicious cycle: rising workloads drive experienced educators out of the profession, placing greater strain on those who remain and further diminishing instructional quality. Across OECD countries, unfilled vacancies are consistently higher in secondary education than in primary, reflecting acute subject-specific shortages in science, mathematics, and technology (OECD Education at a Glance, 2025). Rural and economically disadvantaged regions bear the brunt of this crisis, where limited tax bases prevent competitive salaries and physical distance from teacher preparation programmes compounds recruitment challenges. In Sub-Saharan Africa, the student-to-teacher ratio can exceed 56:1 in some areas (UNESCO, 2024), making personalised instruction a practical impossibility.

1.2 The Limits of Traditional Educational Technology

Previous waves of educational technology have promised transformation but delivered incremental improvement at best. Massive Open Online Courses (MOOCs), which emerged in the early 2010s, democratised access to content but suffered from high dropout rates and a fundamentally passive learning model. Adaptive learning platforms such as early iterations of Khan Academy and Carnegie Learning introduced some degree of personalisation through item-response algorithms, but these systems remained constrained by predefined content paths and rule-based decision logic. They could adjust the difficulty of exercises but could not engage in the kind of open-ended, dialogic interaction that characterises effective human tutoring.

“Students receiving individual tutoring performed two standard deviations above conventionally taught peers — the so-called ‘two-sigma problem.’”

Benjamin Bloom, 1984

The educational technology landscape has long been defined by a gap between the aspiration of personalised learning and the reality of what technology could deliver. The 1984 finding by Benjamin Bloom that students receiving individual tutoring performed two standard deviations above conventionally taught peers established the gold standard for personalised instruction. Yet replicating this level of individual attention at scale remained economically and logistically infeasible for decades. What was needed was a technology capable of understanding natural language, reasoning about student misconceptions, and adapting its pedagogical strategy in real time. The emergence of large language models (LLMs) and agentic AI architectures has, for the first time, made this vision plausible.

1.3 The Case for Agentic AI in Education

The term “agentic AI” refers to systems that exhibit autonomous, goal-directed behaviour, combining capabilities such as planning, reasoning, tool use, and adaptive interaction within dynamic environments (Schneider, 2025; Acharya et al., 2025). Unlike earlier rule-based tutoring systems, agentic AI tutors can engage in Socratic dialogue, decompose complex problems into scaffolded sub-tasks, retrieve and ground responses in verified curricular content, and adjust their pedagogical approach based on real-time assessment of student understanding.

The commercial and empirical landscape already offers compelling evidence of this potential. Khan Academy’s Khanmigo, an AI tutoring assistant built on GPT-4, grew from approximately 68,000 users during its 2023–24 pilot to over 700,000 in the 2024–25 academic year, with projections exceeding one million for 2025–26 (Khan Academy, 2025; DiCerbo, 2025). A 2025 systematic review of 82 peer-reviewed studies on LLM agents in education found five recurring themes: retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) dramatically reduces hallucination; prompt-engineering guardrails preserve academic integrity; multi-agent debate improves accuracy on complex tasks; affective scaffolding increases student persistence; and hybrid human–AI workflows consistently outperform fully autonomous tutors (Garcia-Bosque et al., 2025).

Key Takeaway

The global teacher shortage (44M by 2030), Bloom’s two-sigma problem (1984), and the limitations of earlier EdTech all point to one conclusion: education needs a technology that can engage in open-ended, adaptive dialogue at scale. Agentic AI — with Khanmigo already reaching 1.4M users — represents the first credible candidate.

Part 2

AI/ML Technologies Transforming Real-Time Education

2.1 LLM-Based Agentic Tutoring

At the core of the current revolution in AI-powered education is the large language model, a deep neural network trained on vast text corpora to predict and generate natural language. Models such as GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023), Gemini (Google DeepMind, 2024), and Claude (Anthropic, 2025) have demonstrated near-human performance on language understanding, instruction following, and multi-step reasoning tasks. When augmented with agentic capabilities — planning, tool use, memory, and reflection — these models become capable of sustained, pedagogically meaningful interactions with learners.

The Socratic method, in which a tutor guides a student toward understanding through a sequence of probing questions rather than direct instruction, has proven particularly well-suited to LLM-based tutoring. Khanmigo exemplifies this approach: rather than providing direct answers, it prompts students to articulate their reasoning, identify gaps in their understanding, and arrive at conclusions independently. The Writing Coach module, for example, guides students through essay planning, thesis refinement, and draft revision without generating text on their behalf, and flags passages of more than fifteen words pasted from external sources for teacher review (Khan Academy, 2025).

Technically, these agentic tutors leverage several key advances in LLM reasoning. Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting (Wei et al., 2023) enables models to generate intermediate reasoning steps before producing a final answer, significantly improving performance on mathematical and logical tasks. The ReAct framework (Yao et al., 2023) extends this by interleaving reasoning traces with external tool interactions — searching knowledge bases, executing calculations, or querying databases — allowing the agent to ground its reasoning in verified information. Chen et al. (2024) demonstrated how chaining multiple LLM calls in a tutoring pipeline — one for student state diagnosis, another for response generation, a third for quality verification — can produce tutoring interactions that approach the effectiveness of expert human tutors.

A critical and often overlooked challenge for agentic tutoring is deployment cost. State-of-the-art LLMs contain billions of parameters and require substantial GPU memory for inference, making them impractical for the low-cost hardware that dominates educational settings worldwide — Chromebooks, tablets, and shared classroom computers. On such devices, the binding constraint is frequently not raw computational throughput but the memory wall: the bandwidth bottleneck that arises when transferring billions of model weights from DRAM to on-chip SRAM for each inference pass.

Model compression techniques offer a pathway to bridging this gap. Quantization reduces the numerical precision of model weights from 32-bit floating point to 4-bit or 8-bit integers. Formally, each floating-point weight \( W_{\text{fp}} \) is mapped to its quantized representation \( W_q \) via:

$$ W_q = \text{round}\!\left(\frac{W_{\text{fp}}}{S}\right) + Z $$

where S is a learned scale factor and Z is a zero-point offset defining the mapping between the continuous weight distribution and the discrete integer grid.

By representing each weight in 4 bits rather than 32, quantization reduces the memory footprint by up to 8×, directly alleviating the DRAM bandwidth bottleneck that dominates inference cost on low-power hardware. Recent benchmarks on edge devices show that 4-bit quantized models maintain over 91% accuracy while halving memory usage and reducing inference latency from 150ms to 110ms (Agrawal et al., 2025). Knowledge distillation transfers the capabilities of a large “teacher” model into a smaller “student” model, achieving 85% of the teacher’s performance at a fraction of the computational cost. Structured pruning removes entire neural network layers or attention heads that contribute minimally to output quality, reducing model size by up to 60%.

2.2 Retrieval-Augmented Generation and Knowledge Grounding

One of the most critical challenges facing LLM-based educational systems is hallucination: the generation of plausible but factually incorrect information. In general-purpose applications, a hallucinated response is inconvenient; in education, it can directly implant misconceptions in learners. Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) addresses this by coupling the LLM’s generative capabilities with a retrieval mechanism that fetches relevant passages from a verified knowledge base before generating a response (Lewis et al., 2020).

Formally, RAG reformulates the generation objective as a marginalisation over retrieved documents:

$$ P(y \mid x) = \sum_{z \in \mathcal{Z}} P(z \mid x)\, P(y \mid z, x) $$

where x is the student’s query, Z is the set of candidate documents, P(z | x) is the retrieval probability (via dense vector similarity), and P(y | z, x) is the conditional generation probability given query and retrieved context.

In practice, a RAG-enhanced tutoring system works as follows: when a student poses a question, the system first encodes the query into a dense vector representation and searches a curated corpus — textbooks, lecture notes, problem sets — for semantically similar content. The retrieved passages are then provided to the LLM as context, constraining its generation to information grounded in authoritative sources. Verma (2025) describes LPITutor, an LLM-based personalised tutoring system that implements a modular RAG pipeline: a query processing module extracts student intent, a retrieval module fetches relevant course materials, and a response generation module synthesises a pedagogically appropriate answer anchored in the retrieved content.

The 2025 systematic review by Garcia-Bosque et al. identifies retrieval grounding as the single most effective technique for reducing hallucination in educational agents, with multiple studies reporting significant improvements in factual accuracy when RAG is employed compared to pure generation baselines. Beyond factual accuracy, RAG enables institutional control over the knowledge base: educators can curate and update the corpus to align with specific curricula, ensuring that the AI tutor teaches what the course intends.

2.3 Multi-Agent Systems in Education

While single-agent tutoring systems have demonstrated considerable promise, recent research has explored multi-agent architectures in which several specialised agents collaborate to support learning. Wang et al. (2025b) propose a five-agent system comprising a Gap Identifier (which diagnoses learning deficits), a Learner Profiler (which models student characteristics), a Dynamic Planner (which generates personalised learning paths), a Content Adapter (which tailors materials to the learner’s level), and a Feedback Synthesiser (which consolidates assessment data into actionable insights). Each agent operates with a focused mandate, and their coordinated interaction produces a more nuanced and responsive learning experience than any single agent could achieve alone.

Multi-agent debate, in which multiple LLM agents independently reason about a problem and then compare and reconcile their outputs, has shown particular promise for improving accuracy on ill-structured tasks such as essay evaluation and open-ended problem-solving (Garcia-Bosque et al., 2025). The EduThink4AI framework (2026) extends this approach by implementing a four-agent pipeline where agents for answer generation, validity checking, critical analysis, and final synthesis collaborate using chain-of-thought reasoning, explicitly targeting the development of critical thinking in students.

Knowledge tracing — the task of modelling what a student knows and how their knowledge evolves over time — is another domain where multi-agent approaches have shown advantages. Xu et al. (2024) propose simulating students as different personas, allowing agents to adaptively track knowledge progression. Scarlatos et al. (2025) employ dialogue-driven interactions to probe students’ conceptual boundaries, using conversational exchanges to dynamically refine knowledge estimation.

2.4 Multimodal AI: An Agent That Can See, Hear, and Speak

The most advanced vision for AI in education extends beyond text-based interaction to encompass multimodal perception and generation — systems that can process and produce text, speech, images, and video simultaneously. Recent foundation models such as GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024), Gemini 2.5 (Google DeepMind, 2025), and Claude (Anthropic, 2025) natively integrate multiple modalities, enabling new forms of educational interaction.

Speech recognition and synthesis are transforming how students interact with AI tutors. Real-time voice interaction enables more natural, conversation-like tutoring sessions, particularly valuable for younger learners and second-language (L2) learners. Researchers at the NSF National AI Institute for Student-AI Teaming (iSAT) have developed a Discrete Multimodal Large Language Model (DMLM) specifically fine-tuned for classroom speech recognition, addressing background noise, children’s speech patterns, and accent diversity (Trinh et al., 2025).

Computer vision opens another dimension of classroom intelligence. ClassMind (2025), a multimodal AI framework, combines automatic speech recognition (Whisper for transcription, pyannote for speaker diarisation) with video analysis to generate comprehensive instructional feedback at scale. A systematic review of 104 studies on AI in classroom management found growing use of ML and deep learning for tasks including attendance tracking, behaviour monitoring, and engagement assessment, though only 22% of studies addressed ethical concerns and just 13% implemented privacy-preserving measures.

The integration of vision and language is particularly powerful for STEM education, where learning often involves diagrams, equations, and spatial reasoning. Wang et al. (2025) propose UniEdu, a unified language and vision assistant that combines visual question answering, diagram interpretation, and textual explanation in a single model.

2.5 Toward an Integrated Architecture: The Real-Time Agentic Tutor

The technologies described above — agentic LLM tutoring, RAG-based knowledge grounding, multi-agent collaboration, and multimodal perception — are not isolated capabilities but complementary components of an emerging integrated architecture. A fully realised real-time agentic tutor would combine all four: an LLM backbone capable of Socratic dialogue; a RAG pipeline anchoring every response in verified curricular content; a multi-agent layer coordinating diagnosis, content adaptation, and assessment; and multimodal interfaces enabling the system to perceive speech, visual inputs, and contextual cues.

The shift from generative AI to agentic AI in education is not merely a technological upgrade; it represents a reconceptualisation of the AI tutor from a question-answering engine to an autonomous pedagogical partner.

Chu et al., 2025

The practical implications are already visible. Duolingo Max integrates GPT-4 for contextual conversation practice. Squirrel AI in China has been used by over ten million students, dynamically adjusting content granularity. Research prototypes such as CodeAgent (Zhang et al., 2024b) and Iris (Bassner et al., 2024) demonstrate agentic frameworks for specialised domains like computer science education, integrating web search, documentation retrieval, and code execution into the tutoring loop.

Consider a real-time classroom scenario: a student working on a physics problem speaks a question aloud; the agent’s speech recognition module transcribes and interprets the query; its vision module analyses the student’s handwritten free-body diagram on a tablet; its RAG pipeline retrieves the relevant section from the course textbook; and its language model synthesises a Socratic response that addresses the specific conceptual error visible in the diagram. Each component has matured independently, but their integration into a coherent agentic pipeline is what makes the intelligent classroom possible.

The latency dimension deserves particular attention. Real-time classroom interaction is extraordinarily sensitive to response delay — if a voice-based tutoring agent takes more than two seconds to respond, conversational flow breaks. The end-to-end latency of a cloud-only architecture can be decomposed as:

$$ T_{\text{total}} = T_{\text{edge}}^{\text{infer}} + T_{\text{net}}^{\text{tx}} + T_{\text{cloud}}^{\text{infer}} + T_{\text{net}}^{\text{rx}} $$

Latency decomposition for edge-cloud co-design in agentic tutoring systems.

The architectural response is edge-cloud co-design, in which the multimodal pipeline is partitioned across device and cloud tiers. Lightweight, latency-critical components — speech activity detection, voice-to-text front-end feature extraction, facial engagement classification — run locally on the student’s device, while computationally intensive operations — full LLM reasoning, RAG retrieval, and response generation — are offloaded to cloud infrastructure. By performing audio feature extraction on-device, the system transmits compact feature vectors rather than raw audio waveforms, compressing the upstream payload by an order of magnitude. Edge-cloud collaborative frameworks such as EdgeShard (Zhang et al., 2024) demonstrate how LLM inference can be partitioned across heterogeneous devices, and the NeurIPS 2024 Edge-Device LLM Competition has catalysed research into models that can run on hardware as constrained as a Raspberry Pi 4 with 4GB RAM (Liu et al., 2024).

Key Takeaway

Four complementary technologies form the integrated agentic tutor: LLM Socratic dialogue (with CoT/ReAct), RAG grounding (eliminating hallucination), multi-agent orchestration (specialised roles), and multimodal perception (speech + vision). Edge-cloud co-design and model compression (4-bit quantization retaining 98%+ accuracy) make deployment feasible on classroom hardware.

Part 3

Ethical Implications of AI in Education

3.1 Student Data Privacy

AI tutoring systems necessarily collect substantial quantities of sensitive data about learners: their questions, misconceptions, learning pace, emotional states, and behavioural patterns. When the learners are minors, this data collection raises acute privacy concerns governed by frameworks such as the GDPR in the EU, FERPA in the US, and COPPA for users under 13.

The challenge is not merely regulatory compliance but the fundamental tension between personalisation and privacy. Effective adaptive learning requires rich learner models — detailed profiles of what each student knows, how they learn, and where they struggle. The 2025 systematic review of AI in classroom management found that only 22% of 104 studies reviewed addressed ethical concerns at all. UNESCO’s 2025 report underscores that without equitable, affordable, and sustainable safeguards, AI-powered education risks exacerbating rather than alleviating existing inequalities.

The European Union’s AI Act, which classifies AI systems used in education as “high-risk,” imposes requirements for transparency, human oversight, and data governance. The development of privacy-by-design principles specific to educational AI — including data minimisation, purpose limitation, and the right to algorithmic explanation — is an active area of policy research.

Crucially, the edge computing architectures discussed in Part 2 offer not merely a performance optimisation but a direct architectural solution to privacy concerns. When speech recognition, facial engagement detection, and preliminary NLP are performed on-device, the most sensitive biometric data — a child’s voice, facial expressions, and real-time behavioural patterns — never leaves the local hardware. This approach transforms the privacy calculus fundamentally: rather than relying solely on legal frameworks and corporate data governance promises, on-device processing provides a physical guarantee.

3.2 Over-Reliance and the Erosion of Critical Thinking

Perhaps the most philosophically profound concern about AI in education is the risk of cognitive atrophy. Gerlich (2025), in a study of 666 participants, found a significant negative correlation between frequent AI tool usage and critical thinking abilities, mediated by increased cognitive offloading. A separate study in Acta Psychologica found that greater AI dependence among university students was associated with lower critical thinking levels, with cognitive fatigue partially mediating this relationship.

Unrestricted ChatGPT access during self-directed study produced a steeper forgetting curve compared to unassisted learning — reduced effort during encoding leads to weaker memory consolidation.

“ChatGPT as a Cognitive Crutch,” 2025

The design response to this challenge is not to limit AI’s capability but to structure its interaction to preserve cognitive engagement. Khanmigo’s Socratic design philosophy represents one approach. Lee et al. (2024) propose a guidance-based framework in which the AI provides hints and scaffolding rather than direct answers, preserving the opportunity for higher-order cognition and self-regulated learning. The key insight: the pedagogical design of AI tutors — not merely their technical capability — determines whether they enhance or erode critical thinking.

3.3 The Digital Divide and Educational Equity

AI-powered education has the potential to be either a great equaliser or a great amplifier of existing inequalities. As of 2024, 2.6 billion people remained offline globally, with internet usage reaching 83% in urban areas but only 48% in rural regions (ITU, 2024; ISPI, 2025). The digital divide encompasses AI literacy, linguistic inclusion, and cultural relevance. AI educational technologies are predominantly designed for English, with limited accommodation for multilingual or indigenous contexts (Frontiers in Computer Science, 2026).

A 2024 Brookings Institution analysis articulated an emerging “new digital divide” in which affluent students gain access to both AI tools and human teachers who help them use those tools effectively, while disadvantaged students receive only the technology — without the human scaffolding needed to make it educationally productive. Addressing this divide requires not only infrastructure investment but also subsidised access to AI tutoring platforms, multilingual model development, culturally responsive content curation, and continued investment in the human teaching workforce.

3.4 Academic Integrity

The deployment of LLMs in educational contexts inevitably raises concerns about academic dishonesty. Technical responses include AI-generated text detection tools, which attempt to identify statistical signatures of machine-generated content. However, these tools remain unreliable: they produce both false positives and false negatives, and their accuracy degrades as LLMs become more sophisticated. Khanmigo’s approach — structurally preventing the generation of complete written work and flagging externally pasted content — represents a more robust design philosophy, shifting from post-hoc detection to architectural prevention.

Institutional responses are equally important. Universities are increasingly developing AI-use policies that distinguish between prohibited, permitted, and encouraged uses of AI tools. The most productive frameworks treat AI literacy as a learning objective in its own right, teaching students how to use AI tools critically and transparently. Assessment redesign — oral examinations, process-based evaluation, in-class supervised tasks — complements technological measures by creating evaluation contexts in which AI assistance is either unnecessary or transparently integrated.

Key Takeaway

Four ethical risks demand design-level responses, not just policy: privacy (solved architecturally via edge computing), cognitive atrophy (solved via Socratic scaffolding), digital divide (2.6B offline, 48% rural access), and academic integrity (solved via architectural prevention over post-hoc detection). The EU AI Act classifying educational AI as “high-risk” sets the regulatory baseline.

Conclusions

Toward the Intelligent Classroom

The convergence of large language models, agentic architectures, retrieval-augmented generation, multi-agent systems, and multimodal AI is creating educational tools of unprecedented capability. For the first time, the technology exists to provide every student with a responsive, knowledgeable, adaptive learning companion available around the clock — a prospect that Benjamin Bloom could scarcely have imagined when he articulated the two-sigma problem four decades ago.

Yet the evidence reviewed consistently points to a critical qualification: hybrid human–AI workflows, in which teachers curate, moderate, and complement AI-generated instruction, outperform fully autonomous tutoring systems. The ethical challenges of privacy, cognitive offloading, digital inequity, and academic integrity are real and serious, but they are not inherent to the technology; they are consequences of specific design and deployment choices.

Teachers do not merely transmit information; they inspire curiosity, model intellectual integrity, navigate the emotional complexities of learning, and socialise young people into communities of practice. These functions are not replicable by current AI systems, however sophisticated.

Looking ahead, several research directions demand attention. First, cross-lingual equity: the development and rigorous auditing of AI tutors in underrepresented languages. Second, longitudinal efficacy studies: most existing evaluations span fewer than eight weeks; sustained, large-scale studies are needed. Third, regulatory frameworks: the rapid pace of AI deployment in education is outstripping the capacity of regulatory institutions.

The intelligent classroom is no longer a speculative vision. It is an emerging reality, with millions of students already interacting with AI tutors daily. The task now is to ensure that this transformation is guided not by technological determinism or commercial imperatives alone, but by the educational values of equity, agency, and genuine understanding.

Ultimately, the promise of agentic AI in education is not that it will make teachers obsolete, but that it will make the best practices of teaching — individualised attention, Socratic dialogue, formative assessment, adaptive scaffolding — available at a scale that was previously impossible. The two-sigma problem that Bloom articulated in 1984 may finally have a technological pathway to resolution — but only if we are willing to invest the institutional, ethical, and human capital necessary to walk that path responsibly.
