What If Every Student Had a Brilliant Tutor Available 24/7?

Inside the agentic AI systems that are turning Bloom’s impossible dream — personalised one-on-one tutoring for every learner — into an engineering problem with a real solution.

20 min read ~5,000 words Emerging Topics in Integrated Machine Learning Systems 25/26
Part 1

Education at a Crossroads

TL;DR

The world needs 44 million more teachers by 2030. Four decades after Bloom proved one-on-one tutoring works, we still can’t deliver it at scale — until agentic AI entered the picture, with Khanmigo already reaching 1.4 million users.

1.1 A Teacher Shortage That Keeps Getting Worse

Here is a number that should alarm you: the world needs approximately 44 million additional teachers by 2030 just to achieve universal basic education (UNESCO, 2024). That is not a projection about some distant future — it is six years away, and we are nowhere close to being on track.

The pipeline is collapsing from the other end, too. The 2024 TALIS survey found that one in five teachers under 30 plans to leave the profession within five years; in some countries, that figure rises to nearly half (Education International, 2025). In the United States alone, roughly 411,500 teaching positions were either unfilled or occupied by under-qualified staff in the 2024–25 school year — about one in eight positions nationally (Learning Policy Institute, 2025). These are not abstract statistics. They translate directly into overcrowded classrooms, widening achievement gaps, and students in STEM subjects who may never encounter a fully qualified teacher.

The pattern is self-reinforcing: burnout drives experienced educators out, which increases the workload on those who remain, which drives more burnout. Across OECD countries, secondary schools consistently suffer worse vacancies than primary — especially in science, mathematics, and technology (OECD Education at a Glance, 2025). Rural and economically disadvantaged communities bear the heaviest burden: limited tax bases, geographic isolation, and distance from teacher-training programmes compound recruitment challenges. In Sub-Saharan Africa, the student-to-teacher ratio can exceed 56:1 (UNESCO, 2024). At that density, personalised instruction is not merely difficult — it is physically impossible.

1.2 MOOCs Promised a Revolution. They Delivered a Library Card.

We have been here before. MOOCs burst onto the scene in the early 2010s with world-changing ambitions: top-university lectures, free, for everyone with an internet connection. The reality was more modest. Dropout rates were staggering, and the experience was fundamentally passive — watching videos and clicking through quizzes is not the same as learning. Adaptive platforms like early Khan Academy and Carnegie Learning added a layer of personalisation through item-response algorithms, but they remained confined to predefined content paths and rigid rule-based logic. They could make a problem slightly harder if you got the last one right. They could not ask you why you got it wrong.

“Students receiving individual tutoring performed two standard deviations above conventionally taught peers — the so-called ‘two-sigma problem.’”

Benjamin Bloom, 1984

That gap — between what we know works and what we can afford to deliver — has haunted education for four decades. In 1984, Benjamin Bloom demonstrated that students receiving individual tutoring performed two standard deviations above conventionally taught peers. The result was so striking that it became known as the “two-sigma problem”: we had proof that one-on-one tutoring was transformative, but no way to give every student their own tutor. What was needed was a technology that could understand natural language, reason about misconceptions, and adapt its teaching strategy on the fly. For forty years, nothing came close. Then large language models arrived.

1.3 Enter Agentic AI: From Chatbot to Pedagogical Partner

The difference between a chatbot and an agentic AI is the difference between a search engine and a research assistant. A chatbot responds to queries; an agentic system pursues goals. It plans, reasons, uses tools, and adapts its approach based on what is working. In education, that means a tutor that does not just answer your question — it notices you are confused, traces the confusion to a specific misconception, retrieves a relevant example from your textbook, and walks you through it with Socratic questions (Schneider, 2025; Acharya et al., 2025).

This is not theoretical. Khan Academy’s Khanmigo, built on GPT-4, grew from roughly 68,000 users during its 2023–24 pilot to over 700,000 in 2024–25, with projections exceeding one million for 2025–26 (Khan Academy, 2025; DiCerbo, 2025). A 2025 systematic review covering 82 peer-reviewed studies distilled the field into five recurring themes: RAG dramatically reduces hallucination; prompt-engineering guardrails preserve academic integrity; multi-agent debate improves accuracy on complex tasks; affective scaffolding increases student persistence; and — perhaps most importantly — hybrid human–AI workflows consistently outperform fully autonomous tutors (Garcia-Bosque et al., 2025). The question is no longer whether AI can enhance education, but how to design, deploy, and govern it responsibly.

Part 2

The Tech Stack Behind the Intelligent Classroom

TL;DR

Four technologies are converging into a single architecture: LLM Socratic tutoring, RAG knowledge grounding, multi-agent orchestration, and multimodal perception. Edge-cloud co-design and 4-bit quantization (retaining 98%+ accuracy) make deployment feasible even on Chromebooks.

2.1 The Socratic Machine: LLMs That Teach by Asking

The best human tutors do not lecture. They ask questions. They probe for misunderstandings, offer hints at the right moment, and resist the urge to simply hand over the answer. It turns out that large language models — GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023), Gemini (Google DeepMind, 2024), Claude (Anthropic, 2025) — are surprisingly good at this, once you tell them to do it. Equip an LLM with agentic capabilities — planning, tool use, memory, and reflection — and it becomes capable of sustained, pedagogically meaningful dialogue with a learner.

Khanmigo is the clearest example. Ask it to help you solve a quadratic equation, and it will not show you the answer. It will ask what you have tried so far, nudge you toward the right factorisation, and wait for you to get there yourself. Its Writing Coach module guides students through essay planning, thesis refinement, and draft revision without generating a single sentence on their behalf — and it flags passages of more than fifteen words pasted from external sources for teacher review (Khan Academy, 2025). This is not a gimmick. It is a structural encoding of pedagogical principles into the agent’s behaviour.

Under the hood, several key techniques make this possible. Chain-of-thought prompting (Wei et al., 2023) gets the model to “show its working” — generating intermediate reasoning steps before committing to a final answer, which dramatically improves performance on maths and logic. The ReAct framework (Yao et al., 2023) goes further, interleaving reasoning with tool use — the agent can pause mid-thought to search a knowledge base, run a calculation, or check a database, then resume with that new information in hand. Chen et al. (2024) showed that chaining multiple LLM calls into a pipeline — one to diagnose the student’s state, another to generate a response, a third to verify its quality — produces tutoring interactions that approach expert human tutors.

But there is a catch that often gets overlooked: deployment cost. State-of-the-art LLMs contain billions of parameters. Running them requires serious GPU memory — far more than the Chromebooks, tablets, and shared classroom PCs that most students actually use. On such devices, the bottleneck is usually not raw compute but the memory wall: the bandwidth limit when shuttling billions of weights between DRAM and the processor for each inference pass.

This is where model compression enters the picture. Quantization takes each weight from its original 32-bit floating-point representation and squeezes it into 4 or 8 bits. Formally, each floating-point weight \( W_{\text{fp}} \) maps to its quantized counterpart \( W_q \) via:

$$ W_q = \text{round}\!\left(\frac{W_{\text{fp}}}{S}\right) + Z $$

where S is a learned scale factor and Z is a zero-point offset defining the mapping between the continuous weight distribution and the discrete integer grid.

The payoff is enormous. Representing each weight in 4 bits instead of 32 slashes the memory footprint by up to 8×, directly relieving the DRAM bandwidth bottleneck. Recent edge-device benchmarks confirm this works in practice: 4-bit quantized models retain over 91% accuracy while halving memory usage and shaving inference latency from 150ms to 110ms (Agrawal et al., 2025). Two complementary techniques complete the picture. Knowledge distillation compresses a large “teacher” model into a smaller “student” that captures 85% of its capability at a fraction of the cost. Structured pruning surgically removes entire layers or attention heads that contribute little to output quality, shrinking models by up to 60%. Together, these methods are what make it possible to run an intelligent tutor on the same Chromebook a student uses for everything else — a prerequisite for equitable deployment.

2.2 RAG: Teaching the Tutor Not to Make Things Up

An LLM that confidently tells a student that the mitochondria is the “brain of the cell” is worse than no tutor at all. In general applications, hallucination is annoying; in education, it directly implants misconceptions. Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) attacks this problem at its root by coupling the model’s generative fluency with a retrieval step that fetches verified passages before the model speaks (Lewis et al., 2020).

The mathematics is elegant. RAG reformulates generation as a marginalisation over retrieved documents:

$$ P(y \mid x) = \sum_{z \in \mathcal{Z}} P(z \mid x)\, P(y \mid z, x) $$

where x is the student’s query, Z is the set of candidate documents, P(z | x) is the retrieval probability (via dense vector similarity), and P(y | z, x) is the conditional generation probability given query and retrieved context.

In plain English: when a student asks a question, the system first encodes the query into a vector and searches a curated corpus — textbooks, lecture notes, problem sets — for semantically similar content. The best matches are fed to the LLM as context, constraining it to generate answers grounded in your course materials, not the unfiltered internet. Verma (2025) describes LPITutor, which takes this further with a modular pipeline: one stage extracts the student’s intent, another retrieves relevant materials, and a third synthesises a pedagogically appropriate response anchored in those sources.

The Garcia-Bosque et al. (2025) systematic review identifies retrieval grounding as the single most effective technique for reducing hallucination in educational agents. But the benefits extend beyond accuracy. RAG gives educators institutional control: you curate and update the corpus, and the AI teaches what your course intends — not whatever happened to be popular on the internet during pre-training.

2.3 When One Agent Isn’t Enough: Multi-Agent Collaboration

A single tutor, no matter how capable, cannot simultaneously diagnose a learning gap, design a personalised lesson plan, adapt content difficulty, and assess progress — at least, not well. The solution? Split the job. Wang et al. (2025b) propose a five-agent system where each agent has one focused role: a Gap Identifier (diagnoses deficits), a Learner Profiler (models student characteristics), a Dynamic Planner (generates learning paths), a Content Adapter (tailors difficulty), and a Feedback Synthesiser (consolidates assessment data). Think of it less like a single tutor and more like a well-coordinated teaching team.

An especially promising technique is multi-agent debate: several LLM agents independently reason about a problem, then compare and reconcile their outputs. This consistently improves accuracy on open-ended tasks like essay evaluation (Garcia-Bosque et al., 2025). The EduThink4AI framework (2026) pushes this further with a four-agent pipeline — one generates answers, one checks validity, one critiques, and one synthesises — explicitly designed to model and develop critical thinking in students.

Knowledge tracing is another natural fit for multi-agent approaches. Instead of relying on static test scores, these systems model what a student knows and how that knowledge evolves in real time. Xu et al. (2024) simulate students as different personas to track varied learning trajectories; Scarlatos et al. (2025) use dialogue itself as the probe, refining knowledge estimates through conversational exchanges rather than formal assessments.

2.4 Eyes, Ears, and Voice: The Multimodal Tutor

Text is only one way that learning happens. Students draw diagrams, speak questions aloud, point at confusing passages, and express frustration through facial expressions they may not even be aware of. The most advanced AI tutors are starting to perceive all of this. Foundation models like GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024), Gemini 2.5 (Google DeepMind, 2025), and Claude (Anthropic, 2025) now integrate text, speech, and vision natively, opening educational interactions that were previously impossible.

Speech is the most natural interface for young learners. Khanmigo’s voice mode lets students who struggle with typing engage through conversation. Researchers at the NSF iSAT Institute (University of Colorado) have fine-tuned a Discrete Multimodal LLM specifically for classroom speech — tackling the unique challenges of background noise, children’s speech patterns, and diverse accents that standard ASR handles poorly (Trinh et al., 2025).

Vision adds another dimension entirely. ClassMind (2025) combines Whisper (transcription) and pyannote (speaker diarisation) with video analysis to generate instructional feedback at scale — something human classroom observers can offer only sporadically and at considerable cost. A review of 104 studies on AI in classroom management found growing adoption for attendance, behaviour monitoring, and engagement detection, though only 22% addressed ethical concerns and just 13% included privacy safeguards.

The fusion of vision and language is especially powerful for STEM, where learning revolves around diagrams, equations, and spatial reasoning. UniEdu (Wang et al., 2025) combines visual question answering, diagram interpretation, and textual explanation in a single model — imagine pointing your tablet camera at a circuit diagram and having the tutor explain why your resistor values are wrong.

2.5 Putting It All Together: The Real-Time Agentic Tutor

None of the technologies above works in isolation. The real breakthrough comes from integration: an LLM backbone for Socratic dialogue, a RAG pipeline anchoring every response in verified content, a multi-agent layer coordinating diagnosis and adaptation, and multimodal interfaces perceiving speech, vision, and context. The technical foundations are largely in place. The engineering challenge is orchestrating them with the sub-two-second latency that real-time classroom interaction demands.

The shift from generative AI to agentic AI in education is not merely a technological upgrade; it represents a reconceptualisation of the AI tutor from a question-answering engine to an autonomous pedagogical partner.

Chu et al., 2025

This is not theoretical. Duolingo Max already uses GPT-4 for contextual conversation practice in language learning. Squirrel AI in China has served over ten million students with dynamically adapted content. Research prototypes like CodeAgent (Zhang et al., 2024b) and Iris (Bassner et al., 2024) integrate web search, documentation retrieval, and code execution into the tutoring loop for computer science education.

Picture this: a student working on a physics problem speaks a question aloud. The agent’s speech module transcribes the query. Its vision module analyses the handwritten free-body diagram on the student’s tablet. Its RAG pipeline retrieves the relevant textbook section. And its language model synthesises a Socratic response that addresses the specific conceptual error visible in the diagram — all before the student has time to feel stuck. Each component has matured independently; their integration into a coherent pipeline is what makes the intelligent classroom possible.

The latency dimension is critical. If a voice-based tutor takes more than two seconds to respond, the conversational flow collapses and engagement evaporates. A cloud-only architecture decomposes latency as:

$$ T_{\text{total}} = T_{\text{edge}}^{\text{infer}} + T_{\text{net}}^{\text{tx}} + T_{\text{cloud}}^{\text{infer}} + T_{\text{net}}^{\text{rx}} $$

Latency decomposition for edge-cloud co-design in agentic tutoring systems.

The solution is edge-cloud co-design: split the pipeline between the student’s device and the cloud. Lightweight, latency-critical tasks — speech activity detection, audio feature extraction, facial engagement scoring — run locally. The heavy lifting — LLM reasoning, RAG retrieval, response generation — happens in the cloud. The key insight is that by extracting compact audio features on-device, you transmit feature vectors instead of raw waveforms, compressing the upstream payload by an order of magnitude and taming the most volatile component of latency. Frameworks like EdgeShard (Zhang et al., 2024) already demonstrate partitioned LLM inference across heterogeneous devices, and the NeurIPS 2024 Edge-Device LLM Competition pushed the frontier to models that run on a Raspberry Pi 4 with just 4GB of RAM (Liu et al., 2024). For education, this is not an engineering curiosity — it is an equity requirement. The performance-per-dollar frontier determines which students get intelligent tutoring and which do not.

Part 3

The Hard Questions We Can’t Afford to Ignore

TL;DR

Four risks demand design-level responses: privacy (edge computing as architectural guarantee), cognitive atrophy (Socratic scaffolding over direct answers), digital divide (2.6B still offline), and academic integrity (prevention beats detection). The EU AI Act now classifies educational AI as “high-risk.”

3.1 Your Child’s Learning Data: Who Sees It, and What Happens Next?

To personalise effectively, an AI tutor needs to know a lot about a student: what they struggle with, how quickly they learn, when they get frustrated, what motivates them. When the student is eight years old, that data profile raises questions that go well beyond GDPR, FERPA, and COPPA compliance.

The tension is structural: the richer the learner model, the better the tutoring — but the more dangerous a data breach or misuse becomes. A detailed profile of a child’s misconceptions, emotional patterns, and behavioural tendencies could, if compromised, enable discrimination in future education or employment. The 2025 systematic review of AI in classroom management found that only 22% of 104 studies even addressed ethical concerns — a striking gap given the sensitivity of the data involved. UNESCO (2025) warns that without equitable safeguards, AI-powered education risks deepening the inequalities it claims to solve.

The EU AI Act, which classifies educational AI as “high-risk,” establishes requirements for transparency, human oversight, and data governance. But regulation alone is not enough. Privacy-by-design principles — data minimisation, purpose limitation, algorithmic explainability — need to be baked into the architecture itself.

And here is where the engineering from Part 2 pays an unexpected dividend. The edge computing architectures designed for latency also provide a physical guarantee of privacy. When speech recognition, facial engagement detection, and preliminary NLP run on-device, the most sensitive biometric data — a child’s voice, facial expressions, real-time behavioural patterns — never leaves the local hardware. No legal framework is as leak-proof as data that never touches a server.

3.2 The Cognitive Crutch Problem: Are We Thinking Less?

This might be the most uncomfortable question in the entire debate. Gerlich (2025) studied 666 participants and found a clear pattern: the more frequently people used AI tools, the worse they scored on critical thinking measures, with cognitive offloading — the externalisation of thinking to digital tools — as the mediating mechanism. Younger participants showed the strongest effect. A separate study in Acta Psychologica reached similar conclusions among university students.

Unrestricted ChatGPT access during self-directed study produced a steeper forgetting curve compared to unassisted learning — reduced effort during encoding leads to weaker memory consolidation.

“ChatGPT as a Cognitive Crutch,” 2025

The answer is not to cripple AI’s capabilities — it is to design interactions that preserve the struggle. Khanmigo’s Socratic approach is one model: prompt rather than tell, hint rather than solve. Lee et al. (2024) formalise this as a guidance-based framework where the AI provides scaffolding that still requires the student to do the cognitive heavy lifting. The key insight from the literature is clear: it is the pedagogical design of AI tutors, not their raw capability, that determines whether they sharpen or erode the minds they are meant to serve.

3.3 2.6 Billion People Are Still Offline. AI Could Widen the Gap.

AI tutoring could be the great equaliser — or the great amplifier. As of 2024, 2.6 billion people remain offline. Internet access reaches 83% in urban areas but just 48% in rural ones (ITU, 2024). The divide is not just connectivity: it is AI literacy, linguistic inclusion, and cultural relevance. Most AI educational tools are built in English, for English-speaking contexts, with limited accommodation for the multilingual and indigenous communities that need them most (Frontiers in Computer Science, 2026).

Brookings (2024) articulated what they call the “new digital divide”: affluent students get AI tools plus human teachers who coach them on how to use those tools effectively. Disadvantaged students get the technology alone — without the scaffolding to make it educationally productive. The implication is sobering: simply distributing AI tutors without investing in teacher training, multilingual models, culturally responsive content, and basic infrastructure could make inequality worse, not better.

3.4 Cheating, Detection, and Why Prevention Beats Policing

Students will use AI to cheat. That is not a prediction; it is already happening. The question is what to do about it. AI-detection tools — the ones that try to identify machine-generated text — are unreliable. They produce false positives (flagging honest student work) and false negatives (missing AI-assisted submissions), and their accuracy degrades as models improve and students learn to paraphrase. Khanmigo takes a fundamentally different approach: architectural prevention. It structurally cannot generate complete essays, and it flags anything longer than fifteen words pasted from external sources. Rather than playing cat-and-mouse with detectors, it removes the opportunity in the first place.

Institutions are catching up. The most thoughtful universities now distinguish between prohibited, permitted, and encouraged uses of AI — recognising that a blanket ban is neither enforceable nor educationally productive. The strongest frameworks treat AI literacy as a learning objective in its own right: teaching students to use these tools critically and transparently rather than pretending the tools do not exist. Assessment redesign — oral examinations, process-based evaluation, in-class supervised tasks — creates contexts where AI assistance is either unnecessary or openly integrated. The goal is not to defeat AI. It is to make the learning itself the point.

Conclusions

The Two-Sigma Problem, Forty Years Later

In 1984, Benjamin Bloom proved that one-on-one tutoring could lift student performance by two standard deviations. The result was electrifying — and then, for four decades, it was frustrating. Everyone knew personalised tutoring worked. Nobody could afford to provide it. For the first time, the convergence of large language models, agentic architectures, retrieval-augmented generation, multi-agent systems, and multimodal perception offers a credible technological pathway to that goal. Millions of students are already interacting with AI tutors daily.

But the evidence in this blog points to a critical qualification that should temper any utopian impulse: hybrid human–AI workflows consistently outperform fully autonomous systems. The most effective implementations — Khanmigo, LPITutor, multi-agent educational frameworks — position AI as a tool for teachers and students, not a replacement for the relationships at the heart of education.

Teachers do not merely transmit information. They inspire curiosity, model intellectual integrity, navigate the emotional complexities of learning, and socialise young people into communities of practice. These functions are not replicable by current AI systems, however sophisticated.

Three open research questions will shape what comes next. First, cross-lingual equity: rigorous development and auditing of AI tutors in underrepresented languages, using participatory design that incorporates local pedagogical traditions. Second, longitudinal evidence: most existing evaluations span fewer than eight weeks; we need sustained, large-scale studies to understand long-term effects on learning, motivation, and cognitive development. Third, governance that keeps pace: AI deployment in education is outrunning the regulatory institutions meant to oversee it, and international coordination through frameworks like the EU AI Act and UNESCO’s Ethics of AI Recommendation will be essential.

The intelligent classroom is no longer speculative. It is emerging. The question is not whether AI will transform education — it already is — but whether that transformation will be guided by the values of equity, agency, and genuine understanding, or by commercial imperatives and technological momentum alone.

The promise of agentic AI in education is not that it will make teachers obsolete. It is that it will make the best practices of teaching — individualised attention, Socratic dialogue, formative assessment, adaptive scaffolding — available at a scale that was previously impossible. Bloom’s two-sigma problem may finally have a solution. But walking that path responsibly demands investment not only in technology, but in the institutional, ethical, and deeply human capital that gives education its meaning.
